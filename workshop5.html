You found 1,343 genes. Are they real?

In Workshop 3 you filtered a DESeq2 table and reported 1,343 genes up in bone marrow. Here's the uncomfortable part: you tested about 17,000 genes at once, and at a raw p < 0.05 cutoff, roughly 850 genes clear the bar by chance alone — even if nothing were happening. So how do you know which of your hits are signal and which are the statistics playing tricks?

That question — is this result real, and does it matter? — is the one every scientist has to answer, and it's where most published work quietly goes wrong: roughly half of medical papers that use statistics use them incorrectly. This workshop is the difference between producing a finding and producing an expensive false alarm. You won't memorize formulas. You'll push on the machinery — sliders, buttons, live simulations — until you can feel where it's solid and where it lies.

The stakes. A p-value you don't understand, a correction you skip, a plot you never drew — each turns real lab-hours and grant money into a result nobody can reproduce. Rigor isn't bureaucracy. It's what makes your work worth anything.
01

Distributions & the Central Limit Theorem

Almost every test you'll ever run leans on one quiet miracle: the average of enough independent samples is Normal — no matter what shape the raw data has. Prove it to yourself.

Sample the machine

Pick an ugly parent distribution (top strip). Each draw takes n values from it and plots their mean (middle). Watch those means pile up (bottom) — and turn into a bell curve, every time.

Parent:
Sample size n = 5
Why you care. This is the engine under every t-test, confidence interval, and p-value on your DESeq2 table. It only works when your samples are independent — which is exactly why treating three measurements of one mouse as three mice (pseudoreplication) quietly breaks the math and inflates your significance. The theorem is generous, but it is not fooled for free.

see the Python (optional)

The same idea in a few lines — the version you'd run in a Colab like Workshop 3:

import numpy as np, matplotlib.pyplot as plt parent = np.random.exponential(1, 100000) # any ugly shape means = [np.random.choice(parent, 5).mean() for _ in range(2000)] plt.hist(means, bins=40) # ... it's a bell curve

02

What a p-value actually is

The most misused number in science. A p-value is not the probability your hypothesis is true. It's the probability of seeing data this extreme if nothing were going on. Simulate it.

Run the null — then add a real effect

Each run compares two groups and records the p-value. Start with zero true difference and run a few hundred: the p-values are flat — every value equally likely. Now slide in a real effect and watch them crowd toward zero.

True effect 0.00
The trap. When nothing is happening, 5% of experiments still "pass" at p<0.05. That's not a bug — it's the definition. A single significant p-value is weak evidence; it's outlier-detection on the null, not proof of your idea. This is the seed of the multiple-testing problem in the next section.
03

Multiple testing — the 850 free "discoveries"

Here's where your own analysis lives or dies. You didn't run one test — you ran ~17,000, one per gene. This is the real p-value histogram from your bone-marrow table.

Your bone-marrow genes, all 16,973 tests

Each bar counts genes whose p-value falls in that range. The dashed line is the noise floor — how many genes land in each bin if nothing is real. Toggle the views.

This is the whole ballgame. Raw, you'd report 6,881 "significant" genes — and ~849 of them are noise you'd expect by chance. Benjamini-Hochberg (the padj column you already used) controls that false-discovery rate and trims you to a set you can defend. Skip it and your paper is part noise. Correcting for multiple comparisons is not optional.
04

Significant ≠ important — effect size

With a big enough sample, everything becomes "statistically significant" — including effects far too small to matter. That's why your volcano plot has two axes, not one.

Crank up n on a trivial effect

The true effect below is fixed and tiny (think: 0.3 mmHg of blood pressure — real, but meaningless). Slide the sample size up and watch the p-value dive under 0.05 while the effect never changes.

Sample size n = 20 not significant
Always ask two questions. Not just "is it real?" (the y-axis, significance) but "is it big enough to care?" (the x-axis, effect size / log2 fold change). A gene with a perfect padj and a fold change of 1.02× is real and irrelevant. Rigor means reporting both — and never letting a small p-value stand in for a meaningful result.
05

Look at your data

Summary statistics hide the truth. These datasets have the same mean, standard deviation, and correlation — and look nothing alike. You met Anscombe's Quartet in Workshop 3; here it is, alive.

Same statistics, four completely different stories

Plot before you trust. If these four can share every summary number and still be a line, a curve, an outlier, and a single leverage point, then no table of means and correlations can be believed on its own. This is why you drew the volcano, the MA plot, and the heatmap before reporting a single gene. The Datasaurus Dozen makes the same point with a dinosaur.
06

Spot the fallacy

Bad statistics almost never come from bad arithmetic. They come from a handful of thinking traps that have sunk real studies. Here are the classics — call each one before it calls you.

Regression to the mean — a live demo first

A cohort with no treatment at all, just random noise. Select the worst-scoring 25%, re-measure them, and watch them "improve" — a fake effect that has fooled countless trials.

The rigor checklist

Everything above collapses to a short list of questions to ask of your own results — and anyone else's — before you believe them:

  • Did I look at the data? A plot, not just the summary stats. (Anscombe.)
  • Is it real, or noise? What's the null, and how surprising is this under it? (p-value — understood, not worshipped.)
  • Did I test many things? Then correct for it — padj, not p. (FDR.)
  • Is the effect big enough to matter? Report effect size, not just significance.
  • Are my samples independent? Replicates, not pseudoreplicates. Define the hypothesis before testing.
  • What data don't I have? Survivorship, selection, publication bias.
  • Could a third variable explain it? Correlation isn't causation.
